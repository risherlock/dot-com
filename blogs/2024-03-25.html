Hertz's experiments

In this post we attempt to explore the implications and subsequent validation of Maxwell's equation. In particular, we focus on the ground breaking experiments of a German phycisist Heinrich Hertz who underwent the mission of proving Maxwell's equations in experiments. This blog post is heavily inspired by the book Hertz and the Maxwellians by J.G. O'Hara and W. Pricha. The book studies the influence of the Maxwellians (Oliver Heaviside, Feorge Francis FitzGerald, and Oliver Lodge) on the understanding of theoretical and philosophical aspect of Faraday-Maxwell theory. The influence, however, is not necessarily experimental because he had not started communicating with the Maxwellians yet. Furthemore, this book has helped a lot in understanding the translated works of Hertz which was a valuable resource to explore his experiments.

My goal, however, diverges from that of the book because my intention is to explore the Hertz's experiment alone and to study how it produced the impact that it did. We start from where it all began; the Maxwell's equations, then we analyze the equations and list out the implications of the equations which Hertz would eventually confirm. This will follow by detailed study of Hertz's experiments, the problems he faced, and the conclusions drawn from them. Many experimenters followed Hertz's lead to discover more about the properties of the electromagnetic wave most notably FitzGerald, Trouton.

Maxwell's equations

\(\nabla.\boldsymbol{E}=\dfrac{\rho}{\epsilon_{0}}\)
\(\nabla.\boldsymbol{B}=\boldsymbol{0}\)
\(\nabla\times\boldsymbol{E}=-\dfrac{\partial\boldsymbol{B}}{\partial t}\)
\(\nabla\times\boldsymbol{B}=\mu_{0}\left(\boldsymbol{J}+\epsilon_{0}\dfrac{\partial\boldsymbol{E}}{\partial t}\right)\)

From Maxwell's equations, we know that \(c=f\lambda\) so inorder to validate his theory of electromagnetic waves, standing wave should be produced and product of wavelength and frequency of oscillation of the wave should equal speed of light which Maxwell had showed as the product of magnetic permeability \(\mu\) and dielectric permittivity \(\epsilon\).

Behavior of unclosed circuits in Maxwell's theory. The assumption that changes in dielectric polarization yield electromagnetic effects just like conduction currents was central to Maxwell's theory. Helmholtz wanted experimental verification of the production of such effects or of the converse, namely the electromagnetic production of dielectric polarization.

Propagation of electric waves by means of wire

Procedure to determine the wave velocity in wire.

Propagation of waves in air was found to be faster than that on wire for wavelengths of order 10 meters.

Hertz compared the phased of the waves propagated in air and along the wire and observed that the propagation of oscillation in air was access than that for wire. This observation was not in accordance with Maxwell's theory which suggested tha tthe electrical energy is propagated not by the current flowing in a wire but in the surrounding. He had to use two wires.

The ratio of the speed varied from 1.77 for very fine wires to very nearly unity for thick wires for shortwaves. For 7.5 metres waves, the ratio would be 75:47.

The propagation of wave to be same in both space and wire by introducing two wires does not represent change in the apparatus but whole concept of understanding electromagnetic wave.

Bryant
He was not successful in one area, measuring the velocity of propagation of electrmagnetic waves in air. His arrangements did nt satisfy boundary condition, and therefore could not yield quantitavive results, or at times even qualitative results.

Most frustrating, it is repeatedly reported that a spark generates radio waves. This cannot possibly be true. Only a circuit can do that - when suitably supplied with stored electrical energy to be converted momentarily into electromagnetic energy. In the Hertzian oscillator, or transmitter, the central spark gap merely serves as a very fast-acting switch to discharge the electrical energy stored in the dipole, which acts as a capacitor.
