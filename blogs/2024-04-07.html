Prologue

The launch of Sputnik 1 on October 4, 1957 by then Soviet Union had an immense effect on the technological development of space exploration due to the famous political rivery with USA to win the space. This event started the so called space race which eventually took humanity to the Moon. However, we Nepalese were obvlious to these event for we had own little problems. On 1951, we got independence from 104 years of cruel Rana regime who made sure no public had formal education with the fear that they might revolt against their tereny. The independence was followed by a long stretch of political instability and turmoil with many failed experiments on governance before we settled to current status of federal democratic republic following the constitution of 2015. In short, at the time when world was experiencing the boom of space exploration, we were going through quite a load of socio-political problems, and the ones of orbital mechanics, rocket propulsion, or spacecraft guidance and navigation were not in the list.

The time has changed, young researchers are not only aware of space but are actively engaged in the activities directly related to space and space technology in different forms, including but not limited to popularization and training of CubeSats, R&D of sounding rockets, hosting telecsope Moon/stars gazing parties, academic theses on space technology, or talks and debates in our position in space. Moreover, now we even have our own Sputnik 1; SanoSat-1. Though still in its infancy, we are clearly on our way to explore the space.

On March 2024, SanoSat-1, the first Pico-Satellite designed and built in Nepal by ORION Space Pvt. Ltd., burnt down in the atmosphere after serving its purpose for 2 years. I feel that the story must be told, in its fullest, of the conception, development, and operation of SanoSat-1. It so happens that I was in right time at right place to learn enough of the project which, I believe, qualifies me as a person capable of undertaking the task of writing the story. I joined ORION Space on May 2021, right after the R&D on SanoSat-1 had completed, and was there when it was launched. I have had good fortune of working with the lead engineers of the project. Furthermore, I am well acquainted with the hardware and the software architecture, the details of which I could study and discuss, first hand, with the engineers themselves.

Before I proceed any further on the narration, I find it important to make a note of the audiance I have in mind. I have imagined the readers to be of two classes. First are those who want to know how the SanoSat-1 came to be. In particular, who, why, how, and when did someone plan to take Nepal to the orbit with her own satellite. Second are those who are more inquisitive to the technical details of the satellite including its mission and operation. My goal is to, hopefully, answer to these both classes of readers.

Legacy

SanoSat-1 is by no means the most complex pico-satellite ever built, but it fulfilled its destiny in space. Its success started a chain reaction among young Nepalese students and researchers, inspiring them to think about and push the frontier of research and development in space exploration. In my opinion, this is and will always be the greatest legacy of SanoSat-1.

Epilogue

My association with ORION Space started as my final year project on orientation control of spacecrafts. The fact that I am doing masters in space technology clearly shows that I am a product of SanoSat-1.
